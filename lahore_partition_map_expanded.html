Violence/Significant Sites

The summer of 1947 was not like other Indian summers. Even the weather had a different feel in India that year. It was hotter than usual, and drier and dustier. And the summer was longer. No one could remember when the monsoon had been so late. For weeks, the sparse clouds cast only shadows. There was no rain. People began to say that God was punishing them for their sins.

— Khushwant Singh, Train to Pakistan (1956)

In the Punjab, it may have been half a million killed. Nobody knows; nobody made a list. One incident quoted describes a canal in Lahore that ran with blood and floating bodies.

— Collins & Lapierre, Freedom at Midnight (1975)

The breakdown of the government amidst the communal frenzy is time and again highlighted... The army betrayed administrative orders and took part in the looting and killing of kafilas, making their way across to India.

— Penderel Moon, Divide and Quit (1961)

On 12th August, a big hefty Sikh came riding a motorcycle down Temple Road in Mozang... the local goondas tried to attack him. When they noticed he carried a gun, they dispersed. Half an hour later, another Sikh came—an aged, emaciated carpenter on a bicycle, carrying his afternoon meal. He was pounced upon and stabbed. My mother watched from our window. She could not overcome this tragic incident until her death.

— Dr. Ishtiaq Ahmed, The Punjab Bloodied, Partitioned and Cleansed (2012)

The address I was born at read: "Kailash Gali, Baghwan Bazaar, Amritdhara Building, Gowalmandi, Lahore"—pure Sikh-era nomenclature. Streets named after Sikhs and Hindus were ubiquitous. When Partition occurred, millions of innocent people had to leave their ancestral lands, with armed gangs scouting for easy prey.

— Parvez Mahmood, Children of Gowalmandi, The Friday Times (2022)

Death with all its horrors awaited the unwary on Lahore's roads and bazaars, at street corners, on closed shop fronts. It peeped out of the eyes of Hindus and Muslims and Sikhs.

— Fikr Taunsavi, Diary Entry, August 14, 1947

When India was partitioned, I was in Bombay. I saw the chaos that came... Those were strange days. There was chaos, mayhem, panic everywhere. From the womb of this anarchy were born two nations.

— Saadat Hasan Manto, Essays on Partition

Major Events & Locations

June 21, 1947

The Great Fire of Shah Almi Gate

The largest Hindu business district was deliberately set ablaze. Flames visible from Model Town, miles away. 250+ houses destroyed.

March–August 1947

Mozang — Centre of Violence

Hub of attacks on non-Muslims. Khushwant Singh heard nightly screams from here. Police SHO trained attackers for Gurdwara assault.

August–September 1947

Lahore Railway Station Massacres

"Ghost trains" arrived with massacred passengers. Bodies separated at waist. 700,000+ refugees transported in weeks.

August 15, 1947

Gurdwara Chhevin Padshahi Attack

On Independence Day, the Gurdwara was set ablaze with Sikhs inside. Mozang police SHO masterminded the attack.

September 9, 1947

Gurdwara Dera Sahib Siege

Sacred site of Guru Arjan Dev's martyrdom. ~150 devotees besieged, water supplies cut. Abandoned after weeks.

March 4, 1947

Lohari Gate — First Riots

Junction with Shah Almi, primary flashpoint. A pakora-seller poured hot oil on Hindu students, sparking the cycle.

August 1947

Anarkali Bazaar & Temple

"Anarkali felt like a corpse." A Hindu temple was set ablaze on Aug 17, consuming idols, scriptures, and Vedas.

August–November 1947

Walton Refugee Camp

One of the largest camps. "Little sanitation. Medical aid nominal." Cholera and typhus rampant. 2 chapatis/day ration.

September 1947

Aitchison College

300 boys left in April; only 30 returned in September. "More cows in the dairy than boys in the classroom."

1947

Government College Lahore

Where Khushwant Singh, Kuldip Nayar studied. Its clock tower marked the hour when Shahalmi was set ablaze at 1 AM.

August 1947

Ravi River Bridge Massacre

A convoy of 1,000 refugees from Sialkot was stopped and attacked. "The canal ran with blood."—TIME Magazine

July 10, 1947

Lahore Loco Workshop Attack

7 Hindus and Sikhs killed, 40 injured in a coordinated attack on the North Western Railway workshop.

August 1947

Garhi Shahu Violence

Hindu families near the railway were the first targeted. Ten Hindu homes in a Muslim area—all looted.

July–August 1947

Krishan Nagar Attacked

A planned Hindu colony of professionals. March 15: "Challo Krishan Nagar" cried the mobs. Renamed "Islampura" post-partition.

August 10–13, 1947

Singhpura Burned

A Sikh-majority suburb attacked and set ablaze. Residents opened fire on attackers; troops returned fire.

August–September 1947

Forman Christian College

Two dormitories became 1,000-bed refugee hospitals. The nucleus of today's United Christian Hospital.

August 1947

Lahore Cantonment Station

The only escape route. Sikh soldiers present here made it safer. Main station was "in Muslim grip."

August 12, 1947

Temple Road — Ishtiaq Ahmed's Account

Ishtiaq Ahmed's mother witnessed a Sikh carpenter stabbed to death from their window. "The smell of burning flesh still haunted" witnesses decades later.

1947–1955

Lakshmi Chowk — Manto's Lahore

Where Saadat Hasan Manto lived after migrating from Bombay. Once center of Holi and Diwali celebrations. The Hindu building name was abandoned post-Partition.

August 1947

Gowalmandi — Parvez Mahmood's Story

Refugees from Amritsar found shelter here. Parvez Mahmood was born in Kailash Gali. "Every household had items belonging to that looting."

March 4, 1947

Chowk Mati — First Bloodshed

A Muslim pakorawala poured hot oil on Hindu students; they killed him. Young Muslims from Mochi Gate retaliated. 30 killed, 47 injured.

1947–Present

Valmiki Mandir — Surviving Temple

One of only two functional Hindu temples remaining in Lahore. Many Valmiki Hindus converted to Christianity to stay in their ancestral homes.

August 14–15, 1947

Civil Lines Bus Massacre

A busload of Hindu-Sikh prisoners being taken for exchange was stopped near Temple Road. Driver pretended breakdown. All passengers killed. Taj Din led the attack.

August 1947

Model Town

Upper-middle class Hindu-Sikh locality where author Som Anand lived. The flames of Shah Almi were visible from here. "One never felt threatened—until the troubles started."

August 14, 1947

Rasala Bazaar, Purani Anarkali

Satish Chopra's family fled from here on August 14. "The fire which started from Shah Almi Gate is one of my earliest memories." They carried two steel trunks, a radio, and a sewing machine.

1947

Bhola Pandhe Ki Gali — Yashpal's Lahore

Setting of Yashpal's epic novel Jhootha Sach. A Hindu neighborhood in the walled city whose destruction forms the heart of what critics call the greatest Hindi novel on Partition.

1920s–1947

National College Lahore

Where Yashpal and Bhagat Singh studied and joined the revolutionary movement. Yashpal later used his Lahore experiences for his 1,119-page partition epic.

August 1947

Dharampura

One of the Hindu-Sikh localities burned during August violence. Featured in Yashpal's Jhootha Sach as refugees fled from rooftop to rooftop through connected houses.

Timeline of Violence

March 2, 1947

Khizar Hayat Tiwana ministry resigns. Muslim League agitation succeeds.

March 3, 1947

Master Tara Singh draws kirpan outside Punjab Assembly, shouts "Pakistan Murdabad."

March 4, 1947

First communal riots in Lahore. Hindu-Sikh students protest on streets.

March 5, 1947

Section 93 rule imposed. British Parliament debates: 1,046 killed, 1,167 injured to date.

March 5–7, 1947

Rawalpindi massacres: 2,000–7,000 Sikhs & Hindus killed. News inflames Lahore.

May 18, 1947

Rajgarh attack near Krishan Nagar—first mass slaughter of Muslims in Lahore.

June 3, 1947

Mountbatten announces Partition Plan. Uncertainty over Lahore's fate begins.

June 21, 1947

Great Fire of Shah Almi Gate. Magistrate Cheema directs attacks at 1 AM.

June 27, 1947

Town Hall peace meeting. Muslim League leaders pledge safety. Violence continues anyway.

July 10, 1947

Loco Workshop attack: 7 killed, 40 injured. Bombs thrown on trains at Moghalpura.

August 10–13, 1947

35 simultaneous fires across Lahore. Bharat Nagar, Singhpura, Dabbi Bazar burn.

August 14–15, 1947

Pakistan independence. Gurdwara Chhevin Padshahi burned with Sikhs inside.

August 17, 1947

Radcliffe Award announced. Lahore to Pakistan. Hindu temple at Anarkali burned.

August–September, 1947

"Ghost trains" arrive at Lahore Station. 700,000+ refugees transported by rail.

September 1947

TIME Magazine reports: "One-seventh of Lahore destroyed. 40,000–150,000 killed in Punjab."
